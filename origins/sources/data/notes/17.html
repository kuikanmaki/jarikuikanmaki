Note on Source 5

Discovery of BANAL-52. The late 2021 discovery of BANAL-52 and related sarbecoviruses in Laos confirmed that close relatives of SARS-CoV-2 circulate across the broader Yunnan–Laos region, not just in a single cave system. However, BANAL-52 itself was still too distant to be a direct progenitor. If SARS-CoV-2 emerged from laboratory experiments — such as spike swaps or passaging in humanized mice — the search for a perfect wild ancestor may be futile, as such modifications could create a virus without a directly matching natural progenitor anywhere in the world.

Biosafety Levels in Wuhan Labs

Much of the bat coronavirus research at the Wuhan Institute of Virology was conducted at BSL-2 and BSL-3 levels — lower than typically required for such high-risk pathogens. For more information, see the following Chapter 12 Sources:

  • “The Chinese work was carried out at biosafety level 2 (BSL-2), a much lower tier than Baric’s BSL-3+.” (Jacobsen 2021)
  • “All experiments using live virus was conducted under biosafety level 2 (BSL2) conditions.” (Zeng et al., 2016)

For the U.S. State Department cables warning of poor laboratory security in Wuhan, see Chapter 8. The DARPA DEFUSE proposal also planned to use BSL-2 and BSL-3 conditions (see Chapter 14 Notes).

Analysis of the Early SARS-CoV-2 Lineages and the Market Theory

The chapter discusses early SARS-CoV-2 lineages in Wuhan, showing that none of the earliest known index cases were patient zero, and explores implications for the Huanan Seafood Market as a potential origin site. For research about these distinct early lineages in Wuhan, see the Huanan Seafood Market appendix: Pekar et al., 2022. For more on this topic, also see the findings of the following computational reconstruction study of the progenitor SARS-CoV-2 genome based on thousands of global sequences:

Kumar, Sudhir, et al., An Evolutionary Portrait of the Progenitor SARS-CoV-2 and Its Dominant Offshoots in COVID-19 Pandemic, Molecular Biology and Evolution, July 29, 2021.

The study found that the progenitor genome differs from genomes of the first coronaviruses sampled in China by three variants, implying that none of the earliest patients represent the index case. However, multiple infections worldwide in January 2020 and later carried the progenitor genetic fingerprint, suggesting the virus was spreading internationally before the first reported cases in China. For a clear non-technical explanation of the research method and its relevance to the early Wuhan timeline, see the Huanan Seafood Market appendix: Latham & Wilson, 2022. For more evidence that SARS-CoV-2 may have been spreading worldwide earlier than known, see the section below: ‘Evidence of Earlier International Spread of SARS-CoV-2’. For the first publicly released SARS-CoV-2 genome sequence, see Chapter 1 Sources: Holmes, 2020. For the early Huanan Seafood Market story and the closing down of the marketplace, see Chapter 3.
