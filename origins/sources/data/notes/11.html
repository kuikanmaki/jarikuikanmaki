Scientific American Profile of Shi Zhengli

The June 2020 Scientific American profile of Shi Zhengli (Qiu 2020) was her first major interview in which she publicly discussed the Mojiang miners incident. While it introduced her account of the 2012 events, it omitted several important issues, including undisclosed professional connections (such as Peter Daszak and Ralph Baric, both quoted without mention of their research ties to the WIV), the direct link between the Mojiang samples and RaTG13, the significance of the furin cleavage site, and questions about data transparency and laboratory timelines. These matters are addressed in later chapters (see especially Chapters 11 and 19).

Note on the Furin Cleavage Site

The furin cleavage site in SARS-CoV-2 was first highlighted in Chapter 7 (Coutard et al., 2020). The central scientific question is how the virus acquired this feature. For example, one natural-origin hypothesis suggested that long-term evolutionary pressure in bats could have produced it:

Guo, Hua et al., Evolutionary Arms Race between Virus and Host Drives Genetic Diversity in Bat SARS-Related Coronavirus Spike Genes, Journal of Virology, September 29, 2020.

However, both SARS-CoV-2 and RaTG13 bind more effectively to human ACE2 receptors than to those of bats or other animals — an observation difficult to reconcile with purely bat-driven evolution:

Segreto, Rossana et al., Should we discount the laboratory origin of COVID-19?, Environmental Chemistry Letters, March 25, 2021.

Why, if it evolved in bats, does it bind more effectively to human ACE2 than to bat ACE2? For further reading on the furin cleavage site, see also Chapter 13 Sources (Piplani et al., 2021) and Chapter 17 Sources (Zhan et al., 2020). Both papers were also cited in a classified Pentagon analysis in spring 2020, which specifically flagged the absence of any mention of the furin cleavage site in Shi Zhengli’s 2020 Nature paper as suspicious (see Chapter 19 Sources: DIA, March 27, 2020).
