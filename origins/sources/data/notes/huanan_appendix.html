Appendix: The Huanan Seafood Market

The July 2022 Science papers by Pekar et al. and Worobey et al. argued that SARS-CoV-2 most likely emerged through at least two separate zoonotic spillovers at the Huanan Seafood Wholesale Market in late November 2019, with the market serving as the early epicenter of the pandemic.

Pekar, Jonathan, et al., The Molecular Epidemiology of Multiple Zoonotic Origins of SARS-CoV-2, Science, 26 July 2022.

Worobey, Michael, et al., The Huanan Seafood Wholesale Market in Wuhan Was the Early Epicenter of the COVID-19 Pandemic, Science, 26 July 2022.

These analyses relied on the premise of two distinct viral lineages (A and B) jumping to humans in separate incidents at the market, combined with geospatial clustering of early cases and positive environmental samples near wildlife stalls.

Key Rebuttals

No direct evidence of animal infection or a zoonotic jump at the Huanan Seafood Market has ever been found. No pre-pandemic SARS-CoV-2 positive animals have been identified — neither at the market nor anywhere along the suspected wildlife trade routes. A major Chinese study published in Nature (2023) tested 457 animal samples from 18 species in Wuhan in early 2020 and found all of them negative for SARS-CoV-2. No correlation was observed between occupational animal exposure and infection rates.

Liu, William, et al., Surveillance of SARS-CoV-2 at the Huanan Seafood Market, Nature, 5 April 2023.

The multiple-spillover hypothesis has also been challenged on statistical grounds. Weissman (2026) demonstrated that correcting a fundamental Bayesian error in Pekar et al. reverses its conclusion: using the same data, a single introduction is at least 4.4 times more likely than two separate spillovers.

Weissman, Michael, An Article in Science on Covid Origins Contains a Fundamental Error, Econ Journal Watch, March 2026.

This finding is supported by phylogenetic analysis from Lv et al. (2024), which concluded that all early SARS-CoV-2 variants evolved from a single progenitor (lineage A0) rather than multiple independent zoonotic introductions.

Lv, Jia-Xin, et al., Evolutionary trajectory of diverse SARS-CoV-2 variants at the beginning of COVID-19 outbreak, Virus Evolution, 5 March 2024.

Statistical claims in Worobey et al. (2022) were further questioned by Stoyan & Chiu (2024), who found that the data do not prove the Huanan Market was the early epicenter.

Stoyan, Dietrich, and Chiu, Sung Nok, Statistics did not prove that the Huanan Seafood Wholesale Market was the early epicentre of the COVID-19 pandemic, Journal of the Royal Statistical Society Series A: Statistics in Society, 16 January 2024.

It is important to note that if there were no two separate jumps at the Huanan Seafood Market, the presence of two distinct viral lineages in the market samples could only be explained by an earlier introduction of the virus into humans somewhere else, which would be enough to collapse the market origin theory.

For an accessible critical summary of methodological flaws identified in Pekar et al. and Worobey et al., see also:

Latham, Jonathan, and Allison Wilson, Delete, Deny, and Destroy: Chinese and Western Strategies To Erase COVID’s Origin Are Being Exposed By Independent Research, Independent Science News, 21 April 2022.

Holmes and Andersen’s Authorship of Zoonotic Papers

As noted in Chapter 19, Edward Holmes and Kristian Andersen — the two scientists prompted by Anthony Fauci to write the influential “Proximal Origin” paper (Chapter 6 Sources: Andersen et al., 2020) — were also co-authors on several other major papers supporting a zoonotic origin, including Pekar et al. (2022) and Worobey et al. (2022).

In addition, Holmes co-authored the spring 2020 paper that first promoted the “raccoon dog” theory at the Huanan Seafood Market:

Zhang, Yong-Zhen, et al., A Genomic Perspective on the Origin and Emergence of SARS-CoV-2, Cell, 16 April 2020.

Holmes et al. (2021) also discusses RaTG13 in detail, concluding that it is not the direct progenitor of SARS-CoV-2 and that there is currently “no evidence” that SARS-CoV-2 has a laboratory origin. The authors dismiss concerns about a lab origin as stemming merely from the coincidence of the laboratory’s location in the same city as the outbreak:

Holmes, Edward, et al., The Origins of SARS-CoV-2: A Critical Review, Cell, 16 September 2021.

Notably, at the same time, Holmes himself was listed as a co-author on the partial Ra4991 sequence (later renamed RaTG13), which was submitted to GenBank in 2018 and later claimed to have forgotten about (see Chapter 19). For Holmes and Andersen’s private thinking about the possible laboratory engineering of SARS-CoV-2, see Chapter 6 Sources: Kopp 2023.

Analysis of Social Media Data

A 2020 study used social media check-in data to map hotspots of flu-like symptoms in Wuhan and revealed early clusters radiating from areas outside the Huanan Seafood Market:

Peng, Zhenghong, et al., Exploring Urban Spatial Features of COVID-19 Transmission in Wuhan Based on Social Media Data, ISPRS International Journal of Geo-Information, June 2020.

Importantly, the Wuhan Institute of Virology was located next to the largest identified hotspot during the December 2019–January 2020 period.
