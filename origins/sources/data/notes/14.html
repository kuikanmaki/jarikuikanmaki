Notes to Chapter 14

Note on Source 2

The DEFUSE Proposal. The central document of this chapter is the EcoHealth Alliance DEFUSE proposal, submitted to DARPA’s PREEMPT program in March 2018. The proposal and related materials were first obtained and published by the DRASTIC research collective — an informal network of online researchers — in September 2021.

PREEMPT (Preventing Emerging Pathogenic Threats) was launched by DARPA in 2018 with the explicit goal of predicting and preventing zoonotic spillovers by targeting viruses circulating in wildlife before they could adapt to humans. All page references to the DEFUSE proposal refer to the original document numbering (bottom right corner of each page), which may differ from the PDF page numbers.

1. Aims of the Program

The DEFUSE proposal outlined plans to engineer SARS-related bat coronaviruses by inserting novel cleavage sites into their spike proteins, with the goal of creating chimeric viruses capable of causing SARS-like disease in humanized mice (DEFUSE p. 1).

2. Viruses Targeted by DEFUSE

The proposal focused on bat coronaviruses collected by the Wuhan Institute of Virology and EcoHealth Alliance from a specific cave complex in Yunnan Province, China. Particular emphasis was placed on strains capable of using the human ACE2 receptor and replicating efficiently in human lung cells (DEFUSE p. 3–6).

3. Screening of Collected Viruses

All collected viruses were to be sent to the Wuhan Institute of Virology for initial screening and binding assays before further experiments (DEFUSE p. 10).

4. Introducing Human-Specific Cleavage Sites

The proposal planned to analyze collected bat coronaviruses for cleavage sites and, where appropriate, introduce human-specific cleavage sites (including furin-like sites) into their spike proteins to test effects on viral growth in human cells (DEFUSE p. 11).

5. Experiments with Engineered Viruses and Humanized Mice

The proposal included plans to modify receptor-binding domains of bat coronaviruses and test the resulting chimeric viruses in human airway cells and humanized (hACE2) mice for increased pathogenicity. These experiments were to be conducted at the Wuhan Institute of Virology. Taken together, the plans outlined a high-risk program of gain-of-function research aimed at making bat coronaviruses more capable of infecting and causing disease in humans (DEFUSE pp. 11, 33).

6. Experiments on Wild-Caught Bats

The proposal included plans to capture wild horseshoe bats at the test site in Yunnan and conduct virus inoculation experiments with them at the Wuhan Institute of Virology (DEFUSE pp. 18, 33). All such work with wild bats was to be carried out in China by EcoHealth Alliance staff and WIV researchers. DEFUSE also described live bat colonies at several partner institutions, including Duke-NUS, the US National Wildlife Health Center (NWHC), CSIRO, and the University of Queensland (DEFUSE pp. 6-7, 20, and 30).

7. Biosafety Levels in Wuhan

The proposal explicitly referenced the use of BSL-2, BSL-3, and BSL-4 laboratories at the Wuhan Institute of Virology for various aspects of the planned coronavirus research (DEFUSE p. 25).

8. Shipping Viruses Abroad

The proposal included plans for shipping collected virus samples to partner laboratories abroad in viral transport medium, preserved in liquid nitrogen dry shippers (DEFUSE p. 7). The application also noted prior experience with such international sample shipments (see Note on Source 8).

9. Experiments in Chapel Hill

The proposal designated Ralph Baric’s BSL-3 laboratories at the University of North Carolina–Chapel Hill for chimeric coronavirus recovery, characterization, and rodent experiments (DEFUSE p. 25). Baric was to lead targeted immune boosting work, building on his long track record in coronavirus research (DEFUSE p. 1). See also Note on Source 8 regarding Baric’s letter of support for the related NIH grant.

Note on Sources 5–6

Daszak’s Claims About Bats. In December 2020, Peter Daszak publicly denied that the laboratories he collaborated with — including the Wuhan Institute of Virology — kept live bats or conducted experiments with them. These statements were made while he served as a member of the WHO’s origins investigation team sent to Wuhan. This was despite the existence of the DEFUSE proposal (authored by Daszak), which included plans for bat virus experiments at the WIV (see section 6 above: Experiments on Wild-Caught Bats). For additional context, see also:

Lu, Wei, and Zheng Liu, Shi Zhengli: The Female Scientist with viruses, Sciencenet News, March 10, 2009 (archived).

Note on Source 8

EcoHealth Alliance Year-4 Progress Report. FOIA documents from the related NIH grant show that Shi Zhengli and Peter Daszak had already been shipping virus samples into and out of China for years prior to the DEFUSE proposal (p. 15). The NIH grant documents also mention a letter of support from Ralph Baric (p. 118).
