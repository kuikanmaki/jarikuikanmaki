Scientific Publishing and Censorship

While science censorship in China during the early outbreak has been covered in Chapter 12, similar problems also appeared in Western scientific publishing and media. The peer-review process showed clear signs of publication bias: papers supporting the zoonotic origin hypothesis were often fast-tracked, while those raising questions about a possible laboratory origin faced significant delays, regardless of their scientific merit.

For example, Shi Zhengli’s Nature paper (Zhou et al., 2020) — which contained notable omissions and conflicts of interest — was fast-tracked and published in just nine days. In contrast, papers raising questions about a possible laboratory origin, such as Piplani et al. (2021), faced significant delays (the latter remained under peer review for nearly a year).

Piplani, S. et al., In silico comparison of SARS-CoV-2 spike protein-ACE2 binding affinities across species and implications for virus origin, Scientific Reports, June 24, 2021.

For broader analysis of these dynamics, see:

Birrell, Ian, Beijing's useful idiots, UnHerd, June 8, 2021.

For a detailed chronology of scientific censorship in the early pandemic, see:

Rixey, Charles, Manufactured Consensus: A chronology of scientific censorship within the context of the early COVID-19 pandemic, 12/27/19 - 5/22/20, ResearchGate, September 2021.

Post-Pandemic Rewards

As one example of post-pandemic rewards, EcoHealth Alliance continued receiving major U.S. federal grants even after NIH identified serious reporting and oversight violations in its Wuhan research. One analysis estimated that the organization received over $46 million in new awards during this period (White Coat Waste Project, 2023). For more details, see Chapter 19.
