Note on Source 4

The 2016 Global Virome Project Bellagio meeting brought together several individuals who would later play influential roles in the origins debate. Among the attendees were Peter Daszak, Dennis Carroll, and George Gao. In 2017, Daszak, Carroll, and Gao also attended a China National Virome Project meeting in Beijing together with PLA biodefense officer Cao Wuchun, who served on the advisory board of the Wuhan Institute of Virology (see Chapter 19). Daszak was later appointed to the WHO’s 2021 Wuhan origins mission despite his central involvement in the underlying research; he was subsequently debarred from U.S. federal funding for NIH grant violations (see Chapter 19). Gao became the senior author of a major Huanan Seafood Market study (see Appendix: Liu et al., 2023).

Among the attendees was also Ian Lipkin, Director of Columbia University’s Center for Infection and Immunity and the NIH Center for Research in Diagnostics and Discovery, who later became a co-author of the influential “Proximal Origin” paper (see Chapter 6 Sources: Andersen et al., 2020). Notably, Lipkin stated in a later interview that he had already been informed of a Wuhan outbreak on December 15, 2019. Also present was Dr. Maria Van Kerkhove, who later coordinated the WHO’s SAGO advisory group on SARS-CoV-2 origins (see next note).

Related: Later Developments in the WHO Origins Investigations – SAGO

Following the international backlash to the first WHO Wuhan report, China rejected the proposed Phase 2 investigation in July 2021. The WHO then established the Scientific Advisory Group for the Origins of Novel Pathogens (SAGO). In its final independent assessment, released on June 27, 2025, SAGO concluded that the origin of SARS-CoV-2 remains inconclusive due to missing key data, and reaffirmed that both zoonotic spillover and a laboratory leak must remain under consideration.

Independent assessment of the origins of SARS-CoV-2, WHO SAGO, June 27, 2025.

Notably, SAGO never examined the DARPA DEFUSE proposal or related programs such as the Global Virome Project (GVP), China National Virome Project (CHVP), or USAID PREDICT and their connections to Wuhan. The two largest funders of the WHO — and therefore of SAGO — are the United States and China, the two nations most directly implicated in these programs. As of 2026, SAGO’s position remains unchanged: SAGO members (23 of 27), COVID’s origins: what we do and don’t know, Nature, February 24, 2026.

Note on Source 8

This 2014 paper was cited in “Proximal Origin” (Chapter 6 Sources: Andersen et al., 2020) as evidence that no known virus backbone existed for SARS-CoV-2. The citation is highly misleading: the paper predates the pandemic by five years, yet the authors asserted, based on this source: “However, the genetic data irrefutably show that SARS-CoV-2 is not derived from any previously used virus backbone.”

Note on Source 10

Later Reporting on Undisclosed Variants at WIV. According to subsequent reporting by the Sunday Times, the Wuhan Institute of Virology had been working with undisclosed variants from the Mojiang mine. At least one of these unpublished strains was believed to be an even closer genetic match to SARS-CoV-2 than RaTG13. As one investigator stated: “We are confident they were working on a closer unpublished variant — possibly collected in Mojiang” (Chapter 19 Sources: Calvert & Arbuthnott 2023).

NIH–EcoHealth Gain-of-Function Dispute: Year-4 RPPR and Fauci’s 2021 Testimony

For the full release of the Year-4 RPPR, Year-5 Interim RPPR, and final RPPR for the grant “Understanding the Risk of Bat Coronavirus Emergence,” see Chapter 17 Sources (NIH FOIA library, 2021). Despite NIH’s denials, the experiments described in the Year-4 Progress Report meet the standard scientific definition of gain-of-function research. The chimeric virus replicated to higher levels and caused more severe disease in human-ACE2 mice than the control. NIH later claimed the results were “unexpected,” but the experiment’s design made increased virulence entirely foreseeable. The relevant section appears on pages 485–486 of the Intercept FOIA release (corresponding to pages 161–162 of the Year-4 Progress Report). For later revelations about the NIH-funded experiments in Wuhan, see Chapter 17 (Eban 2021). For more details on what these experiments involved and their significance in virological terms, see Chapter 19 Sources: Ebright 2024. Ralph Baric later testified before Congress that the disputed NIH-funded experiment was “absolutely” gain-of-function research. “You can’t argue with that,” he stated. For further reading, see: Eban, Katherine, Ralph Baric, Whose Virology Techniques Were Used in Wuhan, Testified That Lab Leak Was Possible, Vanity Fair, May 1, 2024.
